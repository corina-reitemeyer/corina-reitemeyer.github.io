Sprint 5 - Core Reflection

Reflecting on the core learning block from the Foundations course.

19 April 2024

My 2 minute reflection on the core learnings

What surprised me about this learning block is how similar and yet how different the skills are that I need as a programmer compared to those from my previous career (and being a digital designer is relatively related to programming). I learned that a lot of things that I have been doing up to date is the product of a mindset I acquired through childhood and school, and the problem-solving skills through the design school I went to many years ago.

What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the core learning?

  1. That I have a very different way of problem-solving currently. I usually go big and wide, explore my options then filter them down towards a selection of solutions that could work. When I did the challenges, I realised that I need a structural, methodical approach and perhaps go experimental when I try little different things, But that experimental mind-set will most likely get me into a rabbit hole if I approach coding problem heads on like that from the start.
  2. I encountered a bit of a fixed mindset and ego in the Tic-Tac-Toe and Dress the Clown challenge. I got majorly stuck with both challenges and I really had to fight the negative voices. When I had to resort to the code along solution for the clown challenge, I felt very unsmart and questioned my abilities. My brain just couldn’t wrap itself around the problem, even in the pseudocode phase I couldn’t fully articulate what I needed to do.
  3. I need to practice more mindfulness to get more resilient towards situations when things don’t pan out my way or I can’t find a bug (which means I end up frustrated). That is not to say I’m completely losing my cool when it happens, but knowing there is a deadline on Sundays, I get a little bit anxious when I’m aware that I don’t have all day for these exercises. Being more collected means I keep a clear head to solve the challenges, and even give myself space to be further curious.

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

They play crucial parts in learning and programming, both individually and collectively - self-awareness is crucial for recognising one's own strengths, weaknesses, biases, and limitations as a programmer. This awareness allows individuals to seek continual growth and improvement. Empathetic programmers are better equipped to collaborate with team members, understanding their strengths, challenges, and motivations. This fosters a supportive and productive work environment, and enables them to anticipate potential problems or frustrations when they are completing a task or on a inter-relationship level. Lastly, values provide a moral compass for decision-making in programming. They guide programmers in making ethical choices, such as considering the implications of their code on privacy, security, and inclusivity. On an individual level, values are also important for the ‘why” of being in this field - what keeps you motivated to keep learning and what inspires you to be a programmer.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?

It surprised me that there was an emphasis on “how to learn” rather than how to communicate, or how to present work. I suppose that’ll come in the bootcamp part, but yeah I don’t think I have ever had an official part in a curriculum where I learned “how to learn” from any education provider I’ve been to.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

Definitely figuring out how it applied to me on a deeper level. Superficially I was aware of how I do things and how I tick, but the nitty-gritty with the practical part was a little bit tricker (and I still feel like I’m in a learning and adopting phase e.g. mindfulness)

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development Bootcamp course?

As someone who has worked along side DA graduates, one thing that stood out is that they can keep their cool if a bug pops up or something else inconveniences them. And they all head a open, curious learning mindset which stood out especially compared to more advanced programmers. It showed to me that being a programmer is not just about technical problem-solving. It’s also how you problem-solve with others, translate a problem from human-language into code, and not shying away of picking up new skills if the current tech-stack would not cover what we needed.

Overall having the core skills block makes sure that we evolve into good problem-solvers, collaborators and learners.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead? Justify your answer.

I don’t think it was a waste of time at all. It encouraged me to look at learning from a different angle. I come from a school system and general environment that encouraged fixed mindsets. It showed me why I felt stuck learning things from time to time (or why it felt bad if I failed or needed more time to learn something). I watched quite a few videos on Youtube around what thinking like a programmer entails which mentioned a lot of the key aspects that the core block has focused on. As a profession with skills that evolves and changes as technology changes, having a healthy mindset on learning is important as we’ll have to learn continuously if we want to keep up with the industry.
