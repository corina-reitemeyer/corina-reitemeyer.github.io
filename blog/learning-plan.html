Sprint 2 - Corina's Learning Plan

This is my plan on how I'm going to conquer all the learnings at Dev Academy.

29 March 2024

What is your long term goal or career pathway?

Within the next 5 years - I would enjoy working as a product developer, front-end developer or Mobile App developer. For me being able to stay on the tools without sacrificing career progression is important to me. In my previous career as a UX/UI Designer, my next step would have been a people & strategy focused leadership role if I wanted to get anywhere further - which would’ve meant hardly any time on the tools. As a practical person, I couldn’t have seen me do this for 8 hours a day, 5 times a week (hence the career change!). Ideally I want to work on different types of tech mediums, to advance my skillset, try different stacks and ways of building things.

Beyond the next 5 years - Further down the track (if in the unlikely case I ever get bored of building apps, websites and other cool things), being a tech expert for accessibility or design systems is also something I’d be really interested in, having already a strong design background in both of those areas and therefore being able to teach or apply my knowledge from both lenses.

However, because I am a keen learner, and most likely end up learning more languages outside of the web development sphere (e.g. Python, C++/#), I can see myself going into a direction that I may not consider just yet (who knows, I might surprise myself and become a game dev when I enter mid-life crisis - even though I am scared that this would ruin my love for video games). I definitely will keep myself open-minded, if roles appear that I may not have thought about just yet.

I can see myself applying my newly acquired programming skills to do some freelance work and side projects as well, where I can use both my design & development skills. It would mean not having to find a seperate developer to outsource that work.

A description of your strengths and limitations when it comes to learning

Learning: Absorbing new information and having “aha” moments is empowering to me. I naturally reach out for finding answers, if I have a question or facing a problem I need to solve, which works well in programming. I have a natural ability to look at the world from a curious perspective. Being able to learn and widen my horizon is one of my biggest motivators, and it gives me joy to give knowledge back to people too.

Focus: Setting goals and milestones is something I do as a part of my day to day life. I can’t start the day and just “do something”. I usually need to have a plan to figure out how to start and when to finish tasks. Without it I feel like a headless chicken, and get overwhelmed easily. So when I work on tasks (in work or in life), I have a plan and a backup plan. Then I have a few mindtrickery - techniques if I don’t feel like doing them (e.g. rewarding myself or telling myself to do something for 5 mins), and usually I end up doing good solid focus sprints.

Discipline: This ties in with Focus, but if I am really interested in something or I have a big goal in front of me, I tend to make sure that I give my best to make it a success and stick with it until it is done. I am quite good at fighting the inner voices that can sometimes try and persuade me to procrastinate or doing something else instead. The only condition on this one is that I really have to want or be interested in something. There is a bit of a grey area, but if I find something really boring or can’t see a good use of my time, it becomes harder to stay disciplined for a long time.

Limitations: Some of my limitations include impatience, overthinking, difficulty improvising, perfectionism and sometimes struggeling with lack of context (which can cause anxiety). In terms of learning, this can affect me in a way where I make it unnecessarily hard on myself and sometimes not realising it early enough. A good example is that I can be quite indeceisive, overthinking my decision and trying to have the "right" answer but not being sure if I do. Which often means that I spend to much time on that rather than actually moving on and doing whatever I need to do, and risking of failing.

What skills (non-technical core/human skills) would you like to see developed in yourself while at Dev Academy?

Definitely want to work on being more patient and working on stress regulation in that regard. I already have some techniques, but I know that there are probably better ways to improve this. Being a better communicator, as I sometimes freakout and then just ramble on, but then not giving people the time to absorb the information or ask questions (so learning good techniques would be cool). Lastly, being more comfortable of being "wrong" or making mistakes (and effectively learning from them), is also something that I could benefit from, as this always gives me great anxiety and I think I'd have a much better time working on things if I knew to be more comfortable being in that space.

A commitment to how you will manage your workload in this programme.

  • I will make sure that I’ll keep tracking my time, and try and stick as closely as possible to the timebox recommendations. I will plan my day ahead of what I want to accomplish and make sure I have some buffer time in between tasks to take short breaks or keep going if I need to finish a task and it takes me a bit longer than expected. If I do need more time, I’ll arrange for my “outside of course” commitments to be taken over by someone else while I finish assignments. If I get stuck with a task despite efforts on solving it myself, I will reach out to people who can help me, either on Discord where facilitators and peers can respond, or if no one is available there, ask one of my developer friends for advice (last resort). I will do so in a polite manner using the guided communication structure that Dev Academy provided.
  • I will make sure I will ask for help when I get stuck beyond 20-30mins of trying to solve a code issue or finishing a task. I will ensure to take regular breaks so that I keep on top of stress regulation and my wellbeing. Planning out my day (if not week) should give me a rough idea how much I can achieve, so if something happens (e.g. I get really unwell) I can reach out to my facilitator on time to discuss options.

A commitment as to how and where you will seek help in a timely way

  • I will try and problem-solve by myself for 20-30mins. This includes trying different methods, solutions, debugging processes and research (e.g. using Google).
  • If that doesn’t work, my next step is to go and ask in the Discord channel, or if I’m on-campus, see if one of my peers or in-person facilitators has time to help me out.
  • If in the (probably rare and unlikely case), everyone is busy, I have around 3-4 developer friends with different programming language skill sets, strengths and experience levels, who I could ask for advice if push comes to shove.

A description of what you expect from the Facilitation team

Being available (doesn’t have to be right on the minute of course, but knowing someone is around is helpful to know), and if they don’t have an answer to a question or problem I’m trying to solve, either direct me to the right person or place, or if they feel like I can figure it out myself and I just looked in the wrong direction, suggest a different approach to try.

Any scheduling information such as block-out times when you are committed to other things. For example, you might have whānau commitments like school drop-offs. It's important to plan these in.

I don’t have commitments during the day, but the only ones that could interfere with my “homework” hours, is my 1 hour teaching at 8pm on Mondays and dance rehearsal on Sundays (4-5:30pm). Both have a degree of flexibility though (aka I can skip it if needed be).

I have a commitment on the 18th of May (afternoon & eve) and Kings birthday weekend, 1-2 of June (all day) that I can’t move. So have planned to make up for the time during the week before and after those weekends.
