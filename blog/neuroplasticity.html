Sprint 4 - Neuroplasticity & Growth Mindset

An exploration on how Neuroplasticity benefits people and why having a Growth Mindset encourages better learning outcomes.

8 April 2024

What is Neuroplasticity?

Neuroplasticity is the ability of our nervous system to its activity in response to positive (e.g. learning a new language) and negative stimuli (e.g. trauma, stress). This means we have the ability to change our brain by creating new neural pathways with consistent exposure to new learned information and experiences. The more the brain is exposed to this stimuli, the further the pathways are broadened and strengthened, making it easier to retain and access the abilities one acquired. The opposite is also true, where the discontinuation of some neural pathways can occur if the person is no longer accessing or using the information or ability they learned (e.g. the feeling of being ‘rusty’ when one has not spoken a second language for a while).

Some of the benefits include:

  • The ability to learn new things
  • Enhance existing capabilities.
  • Recovery from brain injuries and trauma.
  • Improvements to our brain fitness, and also increasing the chance to decrease the risk of certain cognitive diseases.

Some of the ways to increase neuroplasticity are:

  • Learn to play music
  • Learning a new language
  • Playing video games
  • Creating art
  • Physical exercise
  • Mindfulness and meditiation
  • Solving Puzzles

I already some of those principles. I regularly play video games, which helps with motor coordination, visual + spatial recognition, memory and reaction time, decision-making and problem-solving skills. Another would be dancing, which allows me to work on mind-muscle connection, mindfulness (as I have to be fully present to interpret the music to the movements). And exercise such as pilates, which also works on my motor coordination, but also counteracts negative stimuli such as stress.

I quite liked the TEDtalk by Kristen Meisenheimer - not only was it informative, but also very engaging because she used real examples which made me realised how powerful this concept actually is (especially in the realm of overall health & wellbeing).

What is a Growth mindset?

A growth mindset is the belief that one can build upon ones abilities and talents through practice, effort and persistence. It doesn’t mean that everyone will have the same abilities from the get go, but everyone can improve upon what they are starting with. This is different from a fixed mindset, where the talents and abilities are seen are fixed traits, with no space for improvement available. Instead of working on improving ones abilities, a fixed mindset invites for imposter syndrome, where the goal becomes to look smart all the time, and not wanting to risk any mistake for the fear of looking stupid.

This exploration surprised me, because I realised that I have a mixture of both growth and fixed mindsets and those mindsets also shifted around over time. With my dancing (and later on learning to be a teacher), I started with the classic fixed mindset of “I was never a dancer, I’m probably not good at it and have two left feet”. When I proved myself wrong, I was a lot more open minded to growth and pushing my abilities. Now I’ve gone back to more of a fixed mindset again, because I don’t want to look like I don’t know what I’m doing or showing the wrong move when I teach. Having the pressure of being in the spotlight is already not something I’m super comfortable with, and making mistakes in front of people is terrifying. As soon as someone says “wow you’re good at this”, I start fearing that I screw up and I move away from that learning comfort zone. I'm trying to lean back into the growth mindset, mainly by recognising and challenging my inner ego.

With my learning journey as a junior developer, I think I’m still in a safe space where I know I don’t know a lot, and I can embrace a growth mindset more easily - and I’m not teaching anything, I’m merely sharing as a fellow student, there is not that pressure and expectation that I have towards myself. I quite like setting goals for myself, and I like Dweck’s way of how we can use the ‘not yet’ phrase when measuring progress and success.

I particularly found the article on Futurelearn helpful, not only because it gives step by step tips on how to cultivate a growth mindset, but also it has a neat little table comparing an example of how someone with a fixed mindset versus a growth mindset would look at neutral, positive and negative situations. I'm the kind of person who needs practical examples to understand comparisons, and itt made me realise that growth mindsets seem to be more optimisitic and seem less likely to internatilise external feedback, whilst fixed mindsets internalise depending on the type of feedback, which made me think they are a lot more prone to depend on external validation to see how they are tracking (which is how I realised I had a fixed mindset in some scenarios). The difference in self-confidence and how it is perceived by others is quite noticable when seeing the examples.

How do these findings shape my learning plan?

The good news is that neuroplasticity allows us to build upon our exisiting experiences and abilities, given time and effort is put into it, and we can also weaken and undo negative or even harmful ones by disengaging in behaviours and stimuli that keeps those neuro-pathways active. It also means that we can use this concept to change the way we think, our mindset and perspective of the world and how we interact with it.

A growth mindset is compatible with this concept as it encourages consistent learning based on an open-minded perspective towards the situation and the acknowledgement that one can built upon exisiting knowledge. It also means that we have the ability to mold and reshape fixed mindsets, as long as we are aware of them.

Knowing that these concepts are incredibly interlinked allows me now to figure out a strategy to use in my learning journey. I am aware that I sometimes fall into the fixed mindset category (even though I would love nothing else to start from a growth category).

My first step is to recognise and acknowledge where each mindset kicks in, depending on my situation. For me, language is a powerful construct and the way it can have various impacts on me. For example, if I say to myself “I can’t do this” - what it does it puts me in a negative mindset, but strangely it also give me that kick that I need to prove myself wrong. However if someone else says to me, “I don’t think you can do this”, I am more likely to take it to heart and I fall into a full fixed mindset.

When that happens I need to take a step back and ask myself: “How can I rephrase this, and not take it so literally?”

In this case I would rephrase into more positive language, and change it from definitive to more of a vague and open-ended statement. “I can do this, just not yet. I need to practice more”.
