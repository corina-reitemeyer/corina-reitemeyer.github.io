What is a Growth mindset?

A growth mindset is the belief that one can build upon ones abilities and talents through practice, effort and persistence. It doesn’t mean that everyone will have the same abilities from the get go, but everyone can improve upon what they are starting with. This is different from a fixed mindset, where the talents and abilities are seen are fixed traits, with no space for improvement available. Instead of working on improving ones abilities, a fixed mindset invites for imposter syndrome, where the goal becomes to look smart all the time, and not wanting to risk any mistake for the fear of looking stupid.

This exploration surprised me, because I realised that I have a mixture of both growth and fixed mindsets and those mindsets also shifted around over time. With my dancing (and later on learning to be a teacher), I started with the classic fixed mindset of “I was never a dancer, I’m probably not good at it and have two left feet”. When I proved myself wrong, I was a lot more open minded to growth and pushing my abilities. Now I’ve gone back to more of a fixed mindset again, because I don’t want to look like I don’t know what I’m doing or showing the wrong move when I teach. Having the pressure of being in the spotlight is already not something I’m super comfortable with, and making mistakes in front of people is terrifying. As soon as someone says “wow you’re good at this”, I start fearing that I screw up and I move away from that learning comfort zone. I'm trying to lean back into the growth mindset, mainly by recognising and challenging my inner ego.

With my learning journey as a junior developer, I think I’m still in a safe space where I know I don’t know a lot, and I can embrace a growth mindset more easily - and I’m not teaching anything, I’m merely sharing as a fellow student, there is not that pressure and expectation that I have towards myself. I quite like setting goals for myself, and I like Dweck’s way of how we can use the ‘not yet’ phrase when measuring progress and success.

I particularly found the article on Futurelearn helpful, not only because it gives step by step tips on how to cultivate a growth mindset, but also it has a neat little table comparing an example of how someone with a fixed mindset versus a growth mindset would look at neutral, positive and negative situations. I'm the kind of person who needs practical examples to understand comparisons, and itt made me realise that growth mindsets seem to be more optimisitic and seem less likely to internatilise external feedback, whilst fixed mindsets internalise depending on the type of feedback, which made me think they are a lot more prone to depend on external validation to see how they are tracking (which is how I realised I had a fixed mindset in some scenarios). The difference in self-confidence and how it is perceived by others is quite noticable when seeing the examples.
